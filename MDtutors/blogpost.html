How to write a medical personal statement - Part 1: Planning and Introduction

  • Matthew D'Costa
  • 03 Comments

Hi, I’m Matt. I’m a medical student at UCL and in my free time, I work as a private tutor. I’ve been tutoring for about 4 years now and in this time, I’ve started to build a business, help a lot of students achieve their goals and had to read and help edit a bunch of personal statements (especially, for applying to Medicine). The information below is worth reading for anyone thinking of applying to Medicine so please share this with anyone who might need some help with their personal statement.

Step 1- Brainstorm: What is a doctor?

It seems like a pretty obvious answer at first, but in truth, it is probably the most important question you have to ask yourself before writing any application to Medicine. If you, like me, spent your youth growing up watching shows like House, Casualty and Holby City, then it’s pretty easy to fool yourself into believing that doctors fit into this one box: the omniscient hero that goes to the ends of the world for their patients.

The truth is that’s a glorified (and somewhat untrue) image of a doctor. Having a realistic appreciation of what a doctor is can help tailor your personal statement to Medicine and demonstrate a greater awareness of the profession. It is essential that before writing your personal statement, you take the time to list what you think a doctor is and answer two simple questions:

  1. 1. Have I seen a healthcare professional act in this way?

  2. 2. Have I done something that is like this?

Step 2- Opening Line: Everyone’s is the same

It feels like there is a lot of pressure on this one line in your statement. Everyone thinks this is the defining moment where you either demonstrate your worth or are left to pursue your fifth UCAS option (for me, Biomedical Sciences at St George’s). The truth is, sadly, everyone’s personal statement has a very similar opening sentence:

‘I want to do Medicine for x, y and z’

The problem is your reasons for doing Medicine are likely to be very similar to your peers but DON’T stress out. This is a very natural challenge that everyone has when writing their statement and many people find it useful to write the rest of the statement first and then go back to their opening sentence. The only advice I can give you on this section is to be honest and truly reflect on your reasons for doing Medicine.
